# **Sengoku**

## 2–4 players | 45-90 minutes | 12+ years

*As the Daimyo of a clan during Japan's Sengoku period (1467-1615), compete with other Daimyo for territorial supremacy, shaping the board with card placement mechanics inspired by Sudoku puzzles.*

***Historical Note:** The game focuses on Sekigahara (1600), the decisive battle that determined the end of the Sengoku power struggle. Daimyo were regional lords governing domains across Japan. Domain size varied widely, so the main board's spaces are just a gameplay abstraction to represent prefectural borders.*

# **Components**

1 main board  
4 player boards  
4 player decks, each with:  
	1 Daymio card  
	7 Retainer cards (1 exclusive to each deck)  
	12 Prefecture cards  
4 player kits, each with:  
	5 Koku tokens  
	6 Protection tokens  
	1 Market token  
	3 Castle tokens  
	1 Capital token

# **Setup**

1. Lay the **main board** open within reach of everyone, 6x6 side up. **In a two-player game,** instead leave the 5x5 side up.

2. Give each player a **player board** of their choice with the Basic side up, along with the corresponding **deck** and **kit**. Leave any unchosen board, deck, and kit in the game box.

3. Determine the playing order, either by checking the order number of each player board and playing in ascending order, or by randomly deciding on a first player and going clockwise from them.

4. In playing order, have each player place their Daimyo card with one Protection token in one of the main board's spaces with a Daimyo icon.

5. Instruct each player to shuffle the rest of their deck, place it face down nearby, and draw an initial hand of three cards. If a player’s initial hand contains no Prefecture cards, they reveal their hand, reshuffle it into the deck, and draw another initial hand (repeat this until everyone has a valid hand).

# **Definitions**

### **Zone**

Any row, column, or pre-determined area on the main board. On the 6x6 grid, the pre-determined areas are evenly-distributed 3x2 blocks.

### **Control**

A zone is under your control if all of its spaces are occupied by your cards (face up or down).

### **Chain**

Your chain starts from your Daimyo card and includes all cards connected orthogonally to it or to another card already in your chain.

### **Disputed Spaces**

Special spaces inon the center of the grid, marked by the Disputed icon.

***Historical Note:** On the 6x6 and 9x9 boards, the Disputed spaces evoke Kyoto and the Imperial Court, places of imperial influence and prestige, contested by Daimyo seeking recognition and control. On the 5x5 board, they represent a river crossing or narrow battlefield, evoking Sengoku rivalries such as Kawanakajima, where position, timing, and control shaped the struggle for power.*

# **Goal**

Have the most VP (victory points) by the end of the game**.** You score VP by building your Capital before others do, and by expanding your chain with face-up cards, controlled zones, Disputed spaces, Castles, and your Capital. The game ends soon after someone builds their Capital.

***Historical Note:** The title Seii Taishōgun was formally bestowed by the Emperor, providing imperial legitimacy to a military ruler (even when real power was contested). For example, in 1603, Tokugawa Ieyasu received the shogun title from Emperor Go-Yōzei, often treated as the start of the Tokugawa shogunate. On a related note, Akechi Mitsuhide is often remembered as the self-proclaimed "thirteen-day shogun" for his short-lived bid to seize power following the Honnō-ji incidentafter Honnō-ji.*

# **Gameplay**

On your turn, resolve the following steps, then pass the turn to the next player.

1. Perform any number of the following actions, in any order, completing one before taking another:  
   * Play a card from your hand for free, if you have three or more cards in your hand.  
   * Pay 1 Koku to play a card from your hand, if you have two cards in your hand.  
   * Pay 2 Koku to play one Protection token.  
   * Pay 3 Koku to flip face up one face-down card, if it was not played this very turn.  
   * Resolve a rebellion.  
2. Gain Koku according to your income, discard down to two cards (if you still have more than that), and draw cards until you have **three** in hand.

# **Koku**

***Historical Note:** Koku (石) was a real unit tied to rice yield (about 180 liters). It was widely used to measure wealth and a domain’s productivity. A lord was typically considered daimyō-class at 10,000 koku or more.*

When you pay Koku tokens, set them aside away from your supply. When you gain them, take those back to your supply. Each player kit only has five Koku tokens, so any surplus you gain beyond that is ignored.

At the end of your turn, your Koku income is as follows:

* 1 as base income.  
* \+1 for each Disputed space in your chain. **In a two-player game,** only the middle Disputed space provides \+1 to Koku income.  
* \+1 for each controlled zone in your chain.  
* \+1 if your Market is in your chain.

# **Cards**

When you draw cards, do so from your own deck. When you discard cards, place them face-up on their owner's discard pile. If at any moment there are not enough cards for you to draw, shuffle your discard pile to form a new deck, then resume drawing cards.

If two or more cards are **stacked** on a space, only the top card is relevant for effects, limitations, and scoring. Once the top card is removed, the next card in the stack becomes relevant again.

**Retainer cards** have two different abilities for you to choose from when playing one. Each ability indicates the way in which the card must be played, as well as the ability's effect. Check the Retainer Cards section at the end of this rulebook for the complete list.

**Prefecture cards** are those numbered 1 to 6, with each deck containing two of each. When playing one, it goes to the main board in one of the following ways:

1. Face up, placed on any empty space.  
2. Face up, stacked on top of one of your face-up Prefecture cards with no tokens on non-Disputed spaces.  
3. Face down, on any empty non-Disputed space.

If you do not want a Prefecture card you play to cause a rebellion, there should be no other Prefecture cards of the same number on any of its zones. While face down, cards are treated as having no number, and thus never cause rebellions until flipped face up. Once a card is played face down, no one can peek at its front side (not even its owner).

# **Rebellions**

***Historical Note:** The Sengoku period (“Warring States”) was defined by constant conflict and rapid shift in powerpower shifts. The idea of gekokujō (“the low overcomes the high”) captured how underlings could overthrow superiors in this chaos.*

If two face-up Prefecture cards with the same number are within the same zone, that is a rebellion.

### **Preventing Rebellions**

During **anyone's** turn, if a card causes a rebellion immediately upon being played, you can point it out to force its player to either play it elsewhere or discard it. This must be done before that player's turn ends.

### **Resolving Rebellions**

During **your** turn, if you spot a rebellion that cannot be prevented (for example, if both cards causing it were played during other turns, or if one of the cards causing it was flipped face up or uncovered this turn), you can resolve it by pointing out those cards and then choosing one of them to be discarded, along with any tokens on it. As a reward for doing this, play one Protection token.

# **Protection Tokens**

When you play Protection tokens, take them from your supply, and when they are removed, they return to your supply. Each player kit only has six Protection tokens, so any surplus you gain beyond that is ignored.

Played Protection tokens are placed onto one of the following:

* One of your face-up cards on non-Disputed spaces that do not already have tokens.  
* Your Daimyo card, if it does not already have three Protection tokens.

*Cards with tokens of any kind are usually safe from most Retainer card abilities. Note that Disputed spaces are easy targets, since they cannot have tokens of any kind, nor face-down or stacked Prefecture cards (only some Retainer cards can stack there).*

## **Building**

Right after playing a card or a Protection token, you can discard two Protection tokens from cards in your chain to build a **Market**, or three to build a **Castle**. If you have three Protection tokens specifically on Prefecture cards in your chain, building a Castle is mandatory

When you build a Market or Castle, place the corresponding token onto a face-up Prefecture card on a non-Disputed space within your chain that doesn’t already have tokens (it does not need to be one of the cards from which you removed a Protection token).

If you have three Castles in your chain, immediately remove them, flip face down one of the cards that had them, and place your **Capital** token onto it. The card with the Capital cannot be flipped face up or discarded as long as the Capital remains there. Building a Capital triggers the game's end, as explained in the Game End section.

***Historical Note:** During the Sengoku era, castles became administrative and military hubs. Castle towns (jōkamachi) grew around them as concentrated centers of power, trade, and logistics.*

You can only have as many tokens of a kind as those you have inon your kit. If you build something you no longer have in your supply, take one from those you already have on the main board.

# **Game End**

Once the first Capital is built, the active player goes on with their current turn as normal. After that, the Imperial Summons round takes place, giving each player one final limited turn before ending the game (including the player who built the first Capital, who will play the last turn).

## **Imperial Summons**

During this last round, cards are no longer drawn or?nor played. On your turn, do the following actions as many timesmuch as you want, then pass the turn to the next player.

* Pay 3 Koku to flip one face-down card on the main board.  
  * Pay 2 Koku to play one Protection token.  
  * Resolve rebellions.

## **Scoring**

At the end of the Imperial Summons round, the game ends and each player scores as follows:

* If you have a Capital, you score:  
  * 4 VP if you were the first player to build one.  
  * 3 VP if you were the second.  
  * 2 VP if you were the third.  
  * 1 VP if you were neither of those but hadhas a Capital nonetheless.

* Then, score for each of the following **within your** **chain**:  
  * 1 VP per face-up Prefecture or Retainer card. **In a two-player game,** face-up cards on the top and bottom rows do not score.  
  * 1 VP per controlled zone. **In a two-player game,** note that the middle space is a zone of its own.  
  * 1 VP per Disputed space.  
  * 1 VP per Castle.  
  * 4 VP per Capital.

Whoever has the most VP wins\! Ties are broken in favor of the one who first built their Capital. In the rare case that no tied player has a Capital, their factions battle to exhaustion, and whoever getsgot 2nd place wins\!

# **Retainer Cards**

Each of the two abilities in a Retainer card has an icon indicating how it the card is played:

* \[PLACE\] Placed face up on the main board when played.  
* \[TRAP\] Placed face down on the main board when played.  
* \[DISCARD\] Discarded immediately upon being played.

## **Ashigaru**

* \[PLACE\]: Place on an empty space orthogonally adjacent to your chain or on top of one of your Prefecture cards. Then, discard up to two Prefecture or face-down cards with no token around this Ashigaru (orthogonally or diagonally).

* \[DISCARD\]: Draw four cards, choose **one** to keep, and return the others together to the top or bottom of the deck.

## **Chajin**

* \[DISCARD\]: Draw two cards. You cannot do this again this turn.

* \[DISCARD\]: Draw one card. If it is a Retainer card and you play it right away, its number in bold becomes **three**.

## **Ronin**

* \[PLACE\]: Choose a space with a non-Ronin, non-Daimyo card with no tokens, discard up to **one** card stacked there and return all others to their owners' hands, then place this Ronin there.

* \[TRAP\]: When a player flips this face up, you remove up to one Protection token from that player. Then, discard this card and play up to two Protection tokens.

## **Samurai**

* \[PLACE\] Place on an empty space orthogonally adjacent to your chain or on top of one of your face-up Prefecture cards. Then, discard up to **one** non-Daimyo card with no tokens around this Samurai (orthogonally or diagonally), or remove one Protection token from that card.

* \[DISCARD\]: During an opponent's turn, cancel a Retainer card that has just been played face up (on the main board or directly on a discard pile), discarding it with no effect. Then, draw one card.

## **Shinobi**

* \[DISCARD\]: Discard from the main board up to **one** Retainer or face-down card, along with any Protection token it has.

* \[TRAP\]: When a player flips this face up, look at their hand and choose up to two cards for them to discard. Then, discard this card and draw one card.

## **Sohei**

* \[PLACE\]: Place on top any Prefecture, Sohei, or face-down card with no tokens.

* \[DISCARD\]: Take up to **one** card from the top of your discard pile into your hand.

# **Expert Mode**

All rules are the same as normal, with the following adjustments:

## **Setup**

* Use the Expert side of the player boards.

* Keep decks and discard piles on the left side of player boards, to mark that the decks haven't been reshuffled yet.

* When positioning Daimyo cards, players can choose any space that touches just one edge of the board (no corners, no middle spaces) and that does not share a zone with other Daimyo cards. **In a two-player game,** players instead choose any space from the top or bottom rows, excluding those in the middle column and those that share a zone with the other Daimyo card.

## **Destiny Path**

The individual goals listed on each player board grant additional VP at the end of the game if their conditions are met.

## **Emperor Visit**

***Historical Note:** The Imperial Court in Kyoto remained a symbolic source of legitimacy. Warlords often sought court titles or imperial recognition to strengthen their claims. Example: Toyotomi Hideyoshi pursued court legitimacy and became kampaku (Imperial Regent) in 1585\.*

Once per game during your turn, you can shuffle your discard pile back into your deck, moving both your deck and discard pile to the right side of your player board. When you do so, you may also discard your hand before shuffling, drawing three cards anew afterwards. This cannot be done during the Imperial Summons round. If you never do this during the game, add 1 VP to your score.

When playing with this rule, you cannot shuffle your discard pile into your deck in any other way, even if you run out of cards.

## **Alliances**

***Historical Note:** Sengoku politics relied heavily on shifting coalitions. Alliances were often pragmatic and temporary, and could be reinforced through diplomacy (such as negotiated pledges and marriage ties). Many alliances ended in betrayal, including Akechi Mitsuhide's famous defection and multiple Daimyo reversals at Sekigahara.*

Anytime during your turn, you may propose an alliance with another player, so long as the conditions indicated on both player boards are met. If they accept, each of you places one Protection token on the ally's Daimyo card. These tokens only represent the Alliance only, and **cannot** be spent as normal Protection tokens.

Alliance effects apply as defined by the player boards. Additionally, allies cannot flip up each other's face-down cards or target each other’s cards with Retainer effects. You can only have up to two alliances at a given time.

Alliances automatically end during the Imperial Summons round, and cannot be proposed from then on. Alternatively, an alliance might also end if an effect removes one of the Protection tokens that mark it – in such a case, also remove its other Protection token, returning both tokens to their original owners. If you end one of your alliances this way during your turn, claim the reward for breaking an alliance indicated in your player board.

# **5–6 Players Expansion**

All rules are the same as normal, with the following adjustments:

### **Setup**

Use the 9x9 board and the extra player boards, decks, and kits, sold separately.

### **Expert Mode**

The 5–6 player game is often best enjoyed when paired with the Expert mode.
